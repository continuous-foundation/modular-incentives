## Kickoff Meeting Summary

The Reuse Incentives Working Group, convened by the Continuous Science Foundation (CSF) and Creative Commons (CC), held its kickoff meeting to introduce participants, outline the collaboration's goals, and begin exploring how licensing can enable reuse within modular science.

The meeting brought together over twenty participants from across the research ecosystem, including researchers, librarians, publishers, technologists, funders, and infrastructure builders. The group was assembled to address challenges at the intersection of modular research outputs, reuse, and licensing.

## Setting the Stage: From Traditional to Modular Science

The meeting began with an overview of the shift underway in research communication. Instead of knowledge being shared mainly through final papers or preprints, research is conducted by producing modular components, such as datasets, notebooks, figures, code, and protocols.

These modular components can be independently created, shared, and reused at different research stages. However, existing scholarly infrastructure and licensing typically focus on final research outputs rather than the reuse needs and permissions required for modular objects. This creates new challenges in enabling and clarifying reuse within modular science.

Creative Commons highlighted that clear open licensing is essential to ensure that modular research components can be reused, attributed, and integrated across labs, tools, and institutions. Treating reuse and permission to reuse as core infrastructure for science allows research outputs to travel with the signals needed for responsible reuse, including provenance, attribution, and execution context.

Participants discussed how embedded licensing and metadata could allow both humans and machines, including AI systems, to trace and assemble trustworthy research components.

## Introductions and Ecosystem Perspectives

Participants introduced themselves through short breakout conversations. The group represented a wide range of expertise, including:

- computational biology
- open science libraries
- publishing infrastructure
- research data stewardship
- open source ecosystems
- funding and policy organizations
- research software and tooling

Many participants shared that clear licensing and attribution systems are essential to their work, particularly when sharing datasets, notebooks, or intermediate research outputs. Others noted that licensing clarity can determine whether important research tools or datasets can be reused at all, particularly in fields such as health research and software development.

Across disciplines, participants emphasized the need for licensing systems that:

- Reduce ambiguity around reuse permissions
- Support credit for modular research contributions and work
- Enable reuse across platforms and research communities

### The Duck–Horse Problem: An Unexpected Icebreaker

During introductions, participants were prompted with a lighthearted question: "Would you rather face 100 duck-sized horses or one horse-sized duck?"

This lighthearted icebreaker evolved into a thoughtful exchange on strategy, biology, modularity, and ethics.

### Emerging Strategies

Participants generally split into three camps.

**1\. The “One Big Duck” Camp**

Some participants preferred facing a single giant duck rather than managing a swarm of tiny horses. Their reasoning centred on simplicity. One opponent is easier to track, coordinate against, and defeat.

Others argued from curiosity rather than strategy. One participant admitted they would choose the giant duck simply because they were fascinated by what such a creature would look like.

A few participants also brought scientific reasoning into the debate, noting that a horse-sized duck might have difficulty with basic physiology due to the square–cube law, which means its legs and wings might not support its weight.

**2\. The “Hundred Tiny Horses” Camp**

Another group preferred the smaller opponents. Their reasoning included:

- Small animals would be less dangerous individually
- Coordination among many small opponents might be manageable
- Small creatures would be easier to overpower physically.

Several participants pointed out that ducks can be aggressive animals in real life, arguing that scaling a duck to horse size could create a far more dangerous adversary.

One participant who grew up on a farm summarized the argument succinctly:

“Ducks are mean. The only reason they haven't taken over the world is that they are small.”

**3\. The Modular Thinking Camp**

Perhaps the most fitting perspective for this working group came from participants who reframed the question in modular terms.

One participant argued that 100 smaller opponents represent a coordination problem rather than a power problem, suggesting that modular systems commonly break large challenges into smaller components.

Another participant concluded that the small horses are the modular solution, aligning nicely with the working group’s theme.

### Unexpected Scientific Contributions

The debate also generated surprising scientific insights, including:

- Biomechanical constraints of large birds
- Comparisons between giant ducks and hadrosaurs
- The possibility that a horse-sized duck would only be able to waddle slowly

Participants even consulted AI during the meeting to confirm that a horse-sized duck would likely be unable to fly and would move slowly on land.

**A Useful Lesson**

While humorous, the discussion illustrated something meaningful about research collaboration. In many ways, this mirrored the working group's broader goal: bringing together diverse expertise to explore complex challenges in reuse, infrastructure, and the future of modular science.

## Next Steps

The working group will meet across several sessions to examine specific reuse challenges, discuss potential solutions, and synthesize insights into a set of recommendations for modular science reuse.

Future meetings will focus on:

- Identifying real-world licensing friction points
- Exploring infrastructure and metadata requirements
- Developing recommendations for licensing modular research outputs

Participants were also encouraged to read [From Albums to Streams: How Modularity Changes Systems](https://articles.continuousfoundation.org/articles/how-modularity-changes-systems) by Courtney Babott and Rowan Cockett as background reading for the working group discussions.

**Resource notes:**

- creating incentives, licensing systems, and technical tools that enable the practical reuse of interoperable modular research
- normalizing and reinforcing incentivized sharing, reuse, licensing, and attribution of modular research
- developing CC-BY-licensed modular research outputs that are practical to reuse
- celebrating great examples of incentivized sharing and reuse of modular research
