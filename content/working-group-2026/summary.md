We reimagined research beyond traditional research outputs
like papers and preprints, toward emerging modular research
outputs include reproducible notebooks, scientific figures, tables,
data, methods, and code.

We see permission to reuse as core infrastructure that incentivizes
how modular science is shared. Surfacing object-level context,
including licensing, provenance, execution status, and reuse signals,
reinforces confident and responsible reuse, attribution, and
integration of modular research for both humans and machines.

## The Arc of the Working Group

The Reuse Incentives Working Group met five times between February and April 2026, convened by the Creative Commons and Continuous Science Foundation. Each session built on the one before it: surfacing friction, naming needs, consolidating recommendations, and operationalizing them across the levels of a modular research object.

**[Session 1 — Kickoff](./session-1.md) · February 12.** Over twenty participants from across research, publishing, libraries, infrastructure, and funding introduced themselves and established the framing for the group: modular research outputs — datasets, notebooks, figures, code, protocols — are increasingly independent of the papers they once lived inside, and existing scholarly infrastructure largely addresses the paper rather than its parts.

**[Session 2 — Points of friction and incentives](./session-2.md) · March 5.** Breakout groups worked a shared scenario to surface where reuse breaks down in practice. Three themes emerged: researchers need clear reuse signals at the point of reuse, attribution needs to work at the component level, and infrastructure must reduce researcher burden rather than add to it.

**[Session 3 — Naming reuse needs](./session-3.md) · March 19.** Participants articulated **twelve reuse needs** across four color-coded categories — publishing for reuse, setting attribution expectations, reuser guidance, and thinking outside of the PDF — and worked through solutions and incentives for each of six stakeholder groups: sharing authors, reusing researchers, repository owners, hosting institutions, technical developers, and the general read-only public.

**[Session 4 — Refining our guidance](./session-4.md) · April 2.** The twelve needs were consolidated into a draft set of recommendations with concrete tactics, and the group discussed how to sharpen the framing: a stepwise presentation (make it modular → make it licensed → know you can reuse it), a missing machine-readable layer, and the need to connect this work to existing initiatives rather than launch parallel ones.

**[Session 5 — Brainstorming by object](./session-5.md) · April 16.** The final session pressure-tested each recommendation at every level of a modular research object — paper repo, hypothesis headline, panel of evidence, individual text/figure/table objects, and panel dependencies. A single design principle crystallized at the smallest levels: **compliant reuse must be easier than copy and paste.**

## Themes Across the Sessions

Several threads ran through every session.

**Licensing is infrastructure, not paperwork.** Clear open licensing is not a legal afterthought but an enabling layer that lets research objects travel across tools, labs, and platforms — including into AI systems — while carrying the signals needed for responsible reuse.

**Attribution must work at the component level.** Citation practices shaped around whole papers fail to capture the reuse of a figure, a dataset, or a code snippet. Platforms need ways to show citations in context, track reuse per component, and attribute contributions across levels.

**Metadata must travel with the object.** License and attribution information should remain attached to modular content across copies, reuses, and AI ingestion. Anything less incentivizes stripping metadata in the course of reuse.

**The biggest surface is informal.** Much real-world reuse — lab meetings, social media, draft exchanges, AI-assisted synthesis — happens outside formal publishing. The systems for attributing that work are not yet normative, but the appetite is there.

**Existing initiatives matter.** Rather than building parallel systems, the working group emphasized bridges to existing projects and priorities at publishers, preprint servers, funders, and institutions.

## What's Next

The working group produced a [preliminary set of recommendations](./recommendations.md) capturing seven areas of work and the tactics that could operationalize each. A short [next steps document](./next-steps.md) outlines how these recommendations can move from draft to adopted practice — including a proposed OXA RFC on metadata, educational activities, and integration with existing publishing and editing tools.
