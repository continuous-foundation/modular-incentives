The Reuse Incentives Working Group's second session focused on identifying points of friction and incentives surrounding reuse in modular research environments, with particular attention to how licensing can function as infrastructure that enables responsible reuse.

The session brought together researchers, librarians, publishers, technologists, and open science practitioners to explore how modular research outputs, such as datasets, figures, code, and notebooks, might be shared and reused independently of traditional journal articles.

The meeting included a brief recap of modular licensing concepts, a discussion of Creative Commons licensing options, and a breakout session to surface practical considerations for reuse.

Participants were also encouraged to watch the following background resource:

[https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=\_IBTfklg8q0\&t=4s](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_IBTfklg8q0&t=4s)

**Breakout Discussion Scenario**

Participants worked through a shared scenario designed to surface incentives and barriers.

The exercise asked participants to imagine encountering a modular paper repository relevant to their research and to discuss considerations when:

1. Sharing modular components with colleagues
2. Referencing modular components in their own research
3. Creating new analyses using those components

The discussion explored practical questions around licensing, attribution, collaboration, and reuse.

# Breakout Group Highlights

## Group 1 – Licensing clarity and citation practices

Participants in Group 1 focused on how researchers understand the permissions and responsibilities associated with reuse.

Key themes included:

**Clear reuse signals**

Researchers need clear indicators that components can be shared, particularly when work is not yet finalized. Participants suggested mechanisms such as draft vs. final tagging and visible reuse signals.

**Component-level citation**

As research becomes modular, citation practices may need to shift from citing entire papers to citing specific components such as datasets, figures, or code.

Participants noted potential tensions when:

- Datasets are cited independently of the paper that introduced them
- Multiple components require different citation instructions
- Attribution chains become more complex

**Quick-reference licensing guidance**

Participants highlighted the importance of providing simple guidance directly alongside modular objects, such as:

- Licensing permissions
- Citation instructions
- Reuse conditions

This could reduce friction and help researchers confidently reuse materials.

## Group 2 – Infrastructure and incentive systems

Group 2 discussions focused on the technical and incentive infrastructure needed to support modular reuse.

Key areas included:

**Research environments and tooling**

Participants noted that authoring environments and storage systems influence reuse. Questions included:

- Where modular components are stored?
- How they are connected to research workflows?
- How reuse information can travel with them?

**Recognition and metrics**

Traditional metrics such as citation counts may not adequately capture modular contributions.

Participants suggested that new systems could track reuse in ways similar to open source platforms, such as:

- Forks
- Reuse instances
- Contributor views

However, reliance on a single platform was seen as limiting. Distributed approaches and interoperable identifiers were discussed as potential solutions.

**Licensing automation**

Many researchers do not actively consider licensing decisions. Participants suggested that automated attribution and licensing workflows could help ensure reuse conditions are respected while reducing cognitive burden.

**Emerging technologies**

Participants also raised questions about how AI tools might interact with modular research, particularly when models synthesize multiple sources.

## Group 3 – Social practices and collaboration

Group 3 discussions focused on how modular research aligns with existing collaborative practices in research communities.

Participants observed that much early-stage research sharing already occurs informally through:

- Lab meetings
- Presentations
- Internal collaborations
- Draft exchanges

In these contexts, sharing modular components can provide benefits such as:

- Feedback on methods or analysis
- Opportunities for collaboration
- Improved documentation and reproducibility

However, participants also identified several barriers.

**Perceived risks**

Researchers may hesitate to share early-stage work due to concerns about:

- Being scooped
- Sharing incomplete or imperfect work
- Reputational risks

**Provenance and attribution**

Tracking contributions becomes more complex as multiple researchers contribute to modular components.

Participants emphasized the importance of:

- Persistent identifiers (DOIs or other PIDs)
- Provenance information
- Clear attribution signals

**Documentation burden**

Participants noted that documenting modular research outputs while actively conducting research can create additional overhead. Lower-friction documentation practices may be necessary to support widespread adoption.

**Cross-Group Themes**

Across breakout groups, several common themes emerged.

**1\. Licensing must be visible and easy to interpret**

Researchers need licensing information presented clearly and at the point of reuse, with practical guidance on how materials can be shared or cited.

**2\. Attribution needs to work at the component level**

Modular research requires attribution systems that recognize contributions to datasets, code, figures, and other components independently of the final paper.

**3\. Reuse signals should travel with research objects**

Metadata such as licensing, provenance, and reuse indicators should remain attached to modular research objects across platforms and tools.

**4\. Incentives must align with existing research culture**

Researchers are already sharing early work within trusted networks. Modular reuse systems may succeed when they build on these existing practices rather than replacing them.

**5\. Infrastructure must reduce, not increase, researcher burden**

For modular reuse to scale, systems must make licensing, citation, and documentation easier rather than adding additional complexity.

**Next Steps**

The Reuse Incentives Working Group will continue meeting through March and April 2026 to further explore these themes and develop a framework for enabling reuse in modular research.

Upcoming meetings will focus on:

- Licensing structures for modular research objects
- Attribution metadata standards
- Reuse incentives and recognition systems

The insights from these sessions will contribute to a final synthesis document outlining recommendations for enabling responsible reuse in modular science.
